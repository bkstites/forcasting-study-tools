What is the primary purpose of forecasting in supply chain management?
Core Purpose: To predict future demand, enabling better planning for inventory, production, staffing, and resource allocation. Forecasting helps prevent stockouts, reduce excess inventory, and optimize capacity utilization.

Key Applications: • Inventory management (EOQ, safety stock) • Production planning (capacity, scheduling) • Staffing decisions (workforce planning) • Financial planning (budgets, cash flow) • Supplier coordination (procurement timing)

What are the two main categories of forecasting methods?
1. Quantitative Methods: Use mathematical models and historical data • Causal methods (Linear regression, Multiple regression) • Time series methods (Moving averages, Exponential smoothing, Trend projection)

2. Qualitative Methods: Use expert judgment and market research • Salesforce estimates • Executive opinion • Delphi method • Market research

When to Use: • Quantitative: When historical data is available and patterns are stable • Qualitative: When data is limited, new products, or major market changes

What is the general form of a simple linear regression equation?
Formula: Y = a + bX

Components: • Y = dependent variable (what we're predicting - demand, sales, etc.) • X = independent variable (what we're using to predict - time, price, etc.) • a = y-intercept (value of Y when X = 0) • b = slope (rate of change in Y for each unit change in X)

Example: If Y = 100 + 5X, then for every 1 unit increase in X, Y increases by 5 units

Memory Hook: "Y = a + bX" (memorize this exact formula)

Prerequisite for: Slope calculation, correlation, coefficient of determination

How do you calculate the slope (b) in linear regression?
Formula: b = [n(ΣXY) - (ΣX)(ΣY)] / [n(ΣX²) - (ΣX)²]

Step-by-Step: 1. Calculate n (number of data points) 2. Calculate ΣX (sum of X values) 3. Calculate ΣY (sum of Y values) 4. Calculate ΣXY (sum of X×Y products) 5. Calculate ΣX² (sum of X squared values) 6. Plug into formula

Example: If n=5, ΣX=15, ΣY=100, ΣXY=350, ΣX²=55 Then b = [5(350) - (15)(100)] / [5(55) - (15)²] = [1750-1500] / [275-225] = 250/50 = 5

Memory Hook: "Slope = Cross products minus products, divided by X squares minus X squared"

Prerequisite for: Y-intercept calculation, correlation coefficient

What is MAD and how do you interpret it?
Definition: MAD = Mean Absolute Deviation = Average of absolute forecast errors

Formula: MAD = Σ|Actual - Forecast| / n

Step-by-Step: 1. Calculate error for each period: |Actual - Forecast| 2. Sum all absolute errors: Σ|Actual - Forecast| 3. Divide by number of periods: / n

Example: If errors are 5, -3, 8, -2, 1 Then MAD = (5 + 3 + 8 + 2 + 1) / 5 = 19/5 = 3.8

Interpretation: Lower MAD = better accuracy • MAD < 10% of average demand = Excellent • MAD 10-20% = Good • MAD > 20% = Poor

Memory Hook: "MAD = Mean Absolute Difference"

Prerequisite for: MAPE, MSE, CFE calculations

What is exponential smoothing and how does α (alpha) affect it?
Definition: Weighted average giving more weight to recent data, with weights decreasing exponentially

Formula: Ft+1 = αDt + (1-α)Ft

Components: • Ft+1 = Forecast for next period • α = Smoothing constant (0 < α < 1) • Dt = Actual demand for current period • Ft = Forecast for current period

Alpha Values: • α = 0.1-0.3 for stable demand (less responsive) • α = 0.4-0.6 for changing demand (moderate responsiveness) • α = 0.7-0.9 for highly variable demand (very responsive)

Example: If α=0.3, Dt=100, Ft=90 Then Ft+1 = 0.3(100) + 0.7(90) = 30 + 63 = 93

Memory Hook: "Alpha controls responsiveness"

Prerequisite for: Double exponential smoothing, seasonal methods

How do you calculate Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) and what factors affect it?
Definition: EOQ is the optimal order quantity that minimizes total inventory costs

Formula: EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost / Holding Cost)

Components: • Annual Demand (D) = Total units needed per year • Ordering Cost (S) = Cost per order (setup, paperwork, etc.) • Holding Cost (H) = Cost to hold one unit for one year

Example: If D=10,000, S=$50, H=$2 Then EOQ = √(2 × 10,000 × 50 / 2) = √500,000 = 707 units

Factors Affecting EOQ: • Higher demand → Higher EOQ • Higher ordering costs → Higher EOQ • Higher holding costs → Lower EOQ

Memory Hook: "EOQ = Square root of 2DS over H"

Prerequisite for: Safety stock, reorder point calculations

What is the correlation coefficient (r) and how do you interpret it?
Definition: Measures the strength and direction of linear relationship between two variables

Formula: r = [n(ΣXY) - (ΣX)(ΣY)] / √[(n(ΣX²) - (ΣX)²)(n(ΣY²) - (ΣY)²)]

Range: -1 ≤ r ≤ +1

Interpretation: • r = +1: Perfect positive correlation • r = 0.7 to 0.9: Strong positive correlation • r = 0.3 to 0.7: Moderate positive correlation • r = 0 to 0.3: Weak positive correlation • r = 0: No correlation • r = -0.3 to 0: Weak negative correlation • r = -0.7 to -0.3: Moderate negative correlation • r = -0.9 to -0.7: Strong negative correlation • r = -1: Perfect negative correlation

Example: If r = 0.85, there's a strong positive relationship

Memory Hook: "r = Relationship strength"

Prerequisite for: Coefficient of determination (r²)

What is MAPE and how does it differ from MAD?
Definition: MAPE = Mean Absolute Percent Error = Average percentage error

Formula: MAPE = (Σ|Actual - Forecast|/Actual × 100) / n

Step-by-Step: 1. Calculate percent error for each period: |Actual - Forecast|/Actual × 100 2. Sum all percent errors: Σ|Actual - Forecast|/Actual × 100 3. Divide by number of periods: / n

Example: If Actual=100, Forecast=90, then error = |100-90|/100 × 100 = 10%

MAPE vs MAD: • MAPE: Percentage-based, easier to compare across different products • MAD: Absolute units, shows actual error magnitude • MAPE: Better for products with different demand levels • MAD: Better for same product over time

Interpretation: • MAPE < 10% = Excellent • MAPE 10-20% = Good • MAPE > 20% = Poor

Memory Hook: "MAPE = Mean Absolute Percent Error"

Prerequisite for: MSE, CFE, forecast accuracy comparison

What does the coefficient of determination (r²) represent?
Definition: r² represents the proportion of variation in the dependent variable (Y) that is explained by the regression model

Formula: r² = (Explained Variation) / (Total Variation)

Range: 0 ≤ r² ≤ 1 (or 0% to 100%)

Interpretation: • r² = 0.90 (90%): 90% of variation in Y is explained by X • r² = 0.50 (50%): 50% of variation in Y is explained by X • r² = 0.10 (10%): Only 10% of variation in Y is explained by X

Example: If r² = 0.75, then 75% of the variation in demand is explained by the independent variable (like time or price)

Memory Hook: "r² = How much variation is explained"

Prerequisite for: Model evaluation, forecast confidence

When should you use a moving average forecasting method?
Best Use Cases: Moving averages work best for stable demand patterns with no clear trend or seasonality

Characteristics of Suitable Data: • Demand is relatively stable over time • No clear upward or downward trend • No strong seasonal patterns • Random fluctuations around a mean

When NOT to Use: • Clear trend (increasing or decreasing) • Strong seasonality • New products with limited history • Highly variable demand

Example: Monthly utility bills, basic office supplies, stable consumer goods

Memory Hook: "Moving Average = Stable, No Trend"

Prerequisite for: Exponential smoothing, trend analysis

What is a seasonal factor in seasonal forecasting?
Definition: A seasonal factor is the ratio of actual seasonal demand to the average demand per season

Formula: Seasonal Factor = (Actual Seasonal Demand) / (Average Demand)

Calculation Steps: 1. Calculate average demand across all seasons 2. For each season, divide actual demand by average 3. Seasonal factors should average to 1.0

Example: If average monthly demand is 100 units: • January: 80 units → Seasonal factor = 80/100 = 0.8 • December: 150 units → Seasonal factor = 150/100 = 1.5

Interpretation: • Factor > 1.0: Above average demand • Factor < 1.0: Below average demand • Factor = 1.0: Average demand

Memory Hook: "Seasonal Factor = Actual / Average"

Prerequisite for: Seasonal adjustment, multiplicative methods

What is the relationship between α and n in exponential smoothing vs. moving averages?
Approximate Relationship: α ≈ 2/(n+1)

Where: • α = smoothing constant in exponential smoothing • n = number of periods in moving average

Examples: • n=3 (3-period MA) → α ≈ 2/(3+1) = 0.5 • n=5 (5-period MA) → α ≈ 2/(5+1) = 0.33 • n=10 (10-period MA) → α ≈ 2/(10+1) = 0.18

Interpretation: • Higher n (more periods) → Lower α (less responsive) • Lower n (fewer periods) → Higher α (more responsive)

Memory Hook: "Alpha = 2 over n plus 1"

Prerequisite for: Method selection, parameter tuning
